LUMATICAL

Light is your instrument

A physics-based puzzle game where you bend, split, and direct beams of light through mirrors, prisms, and lenses to solve increasingly mind-bending challenges. No narrative. Pure luminescence. Pure logic.

01 — Vision

The Core Idea

Lumatical is a game about the pure, visceral pleasure of controlling light. There is no story to follow, no lore to uncover — only the light, the grid, and your mind. Each puzzle presents a source beam and a set of targets, and your job is to bridge the gap using a curated set of optical tools. The satisfaction comes from the click: the moment your arrangement clicks into place and every beam lands on its target, painting the dark grid in vivid neon.


The game draws inspiration from the elegant constraint of games like Baba Is You and Stephen's Sausage Roll, but trades their opaque rule systems for something immediately intuitive — everyone understands what a mirror does. The depth comes not from learning rules, but from the combinatorial explosion of how simple tools interact. A prism splitting white light into RGB. A lens bending a beam just enough to hit a mirror that sends it through a second prism. A filter that removes one color from a beam, letting the rest pass through. The puzzles start simple. They do not stay simple.


The aesthetic direction — neon sign light beams, bloom-saturated dark voids, subtly breathing backgrounds — is not decoration. It is the point. The game feels like controlling light. Every solved puzzle is a small, private light show. The generative audio system ensures that show has a soundtrack no one else has ever heard.

02 — Gameplay

How It Plays

Every puzzle in Lumatical takes place on an isometric grid. The grid contains fixed elements — a light source, one or more targets, and optional obstacles or enemies — and the player is given a limited set of tools to place. The core loop is: observe the puzzle, plan your arrangement, place your tools, and fire the beam. If all targets are hit with the correct color and intensity, the puzzle is solved. If not, you adjust and try again. There is no penalty for experimentation — the light simply resets when you move a tool.


The interaction model is drag-and-drop. On PC, you click a tool from your toolbelt at the bottom of the screen and drag it onto the grid. On mobile, you tap and drag with touch. Tools snap to grid intersections (or grid cells, depending on the tool type) and can be rotated after placement by tapping/clicking them. A beam preview shows the projected path of the light in real-time as you place and rotate tools, giving you instant visual feedback before you commit. This preview is dimmed and translucent — the full-brightness beam only appears when you press the "Fire" button or when the puzzle auto-fires on tool placement (configurable in settings).

What makes Lumatical puzzles interesting is constraint. The player rarely has unlimited tools — each puzzle specifies exactly how many mirrors, prisms, and lenses are available. This turns every puzzle into an optimization problem: can you reach all targets with the tools you have? Early puzzles might give you three mirrors when you only need two (teaching the mechanic), while later puzzles might give you exactly the number you need with zero margin for error, or even fewer than seems possible — forcing you to discover non-obvious interactions between tools.

03 — Tools

Instruments of Light

Every tool in Lumatical is based on real optical physics, simplified just enough to be intuitive. The physics engine tracks each beam's color, intensity, direction, and width — and every tool modifies these properties in specific, predictable ways. Mastery comes from understanding how tools chain together.

M

Mirror

Reflects any beam that hits it at an angle equal to the angle of incidence. Can be rotated to any of 8 positions (45-degree increments). A beam hitting a mirror head-on passes through. Mirrors preserve the color and intensity of the reflected beam. They are the most basic and versatile tool — most puzzles will give you several. Advanced use: two mirrors can create a U-turn, redirecting a beam back along its original path but offset by the distance between them.

P

Prism

Splits a white beam into its component colors (red, green, blue), each diverging at a fixed angular separation. Colored beams pass through a prism unaffected. The prism's orientation determines the plane of divergence — rotating it changes which direction the split beams fan out. This is the tool that introduces color as a puzzle dimension. Later puzzles feature multi-prism setups where a split beam is recombined, or where one component is filtered out and the remaining two are split again through a second prism angled differently.

L

Lens

Bends a beam's path by a fixed angle (converging or diverging) without changing its color. Lenses can be used to nudge a beam slightly — sometimes that 15-degree bend is exactly what you need to thread a beam between two obstacles. Convex lenses focus a beam (narrowing its width, increasing intensity), while concave lenses spread it (widening the beam, reducing intensity). Intensity matters: some targets require a minimum intensity to activate, and some enemies can only be destroyed by a focused beam.

F

Color Filter

Allows only one specific color to pass through; the rest of the beam is absorbed. Place a red filter in a white beam's path, and only the red component continues — green and blue are blocked. This is essential for puzzles with multiple colored targets that are close together, where you need to route each color independently. Filters are single-use per beam: a red filter on an already-red beam has no effect.

S

Beam Splitter

Duplicates a beam into two identical copies at a fixed angular separation. Unlike a prism (which separates colors), a splitter produces two beams of the same color and intensity. This is the tool for when you need to hit two targets of the same color but only have one beam. The trade-off: each split beam has half the intensity of the original. Chain too many splitters and the beams become too weak to activate targets — a natural limiter that creates interesting constraint puzzles.

B

Blocker

A fixed obstacle that fully absorbs any beam. Blockers are part of the puzzle layout — you cannot move or remove them. They force creative routing around obstacles. Some blockers are one-way (they block beams from one direction but allow them through from the opposite side), adding spatial reasoning to the puzzle. Enemy-type blockers absorb beams and must be destroyed by hitting them with the correct color — turning the blocker from an obstacle into a target.

⚠ Open Design Question

Should we include any additional tools for later chapters? Candidates under consideration:

  • Refractor Cube — Bends a beam 90 degrees regardless of entry angle. Simpler than a mirror but less flexible.
  • Color Mixer — Reverse-prism. Merges two colored beams into one (red + blue = magenta output). Opens composite-color puzzles.
  • Timer Gate — Opens/closes on a cycle, requiring the player to time when the beam reaches it. Adds a temporal dimension.
  • Teleporter — Beam enters one portal and exits another at the same angle. Useful for wrapping beams across large grids.

These would be introduced in Chapters V and VI if included, keeping early chapters focused on the core six tools.

04 — Aesthetics

Look & Feel

Lumatical's visual identity lives at the intersection of neon signage, retro-futurism, and minimal surrealism. The screen is overwhelmingly dark — deep, near-black backgrounds that make the light beams the absolute star of every frame. The beams themselves glow like neon tubes: soft, bloom-heavy, with subtle chromatic aberration at their edges. When a beam hits a mirror, there is a brief flash and a soft lens flare. When a prism splits white into RGB, the bloom cascade is almost overwhelming for a moment before settling. The game wants you to feel the light.


The 2.5D isometric perspective gives the grid depth without complexity. The grid surface is a subtly translucent plane — you can see faint geometric patterns beneath it, like looking through frosted glass at circuitry. Tool icons sit slightly raised above the grid surface with soft drop shadows, making them feel physical and tactile despite the minimalist style.


Backgrounds are not static. Each chapter has a slowly morphing abstract backdrop — shifting gradients, very slow particle drift, barely perceptible geometric shapes that breathe in and out. These are hypnotic and atmospheric, never distracting. The effect should feel like staring into a deep, dark aquarium where something is always moving but you can never quite focus on it.

Subtle RGB channel offset on beam edges. More pronounced at beam split points and during the solve animation. Not a constant effect — it pulses gently, giving the scene a living, breathing quality.

Screen-Space Reflections

The grid surface has a slight reflectivity. Beams cast soft reflections beneath them, doubling the visual density. Mirror surfaces show a bright, focused reflection of incoming beams.

Particle Drift

Faint, slow-moving particles in the background. Near-invisible until a beam passes through them — then they light up briefly like dust motes caught in a flashlight. Adds subtle depth and atmosphere.

✓ Design Decision — Vector / Programmatic Assets

All game assets will be vector/programmatic rather than raster/bitmapped. This is a strategic choice with several benefits: (1) pixel-perfect rendering at any resolution, from mobile screens to 4K displays, with zero asset duplication; (2) drastically smaller file sizes — the entire art pipeline is code, not texture atlases; (3) easier to implement visual effects modifications at runtime (changing beam width, glow intensity, color tinting); (4) simpler porting across platforms since there are no platform-specific texture compression formats to worry about. Godot's built-in drawing primitives and shader language make this approach natural.

05 — Audio

Generative Sound

Lumatical's audio system is not a soundtrack — it is an instrument the player plays by solving puzzles. The core concept is generative audio: each beam of light produces a sustained tone, and the tone's properties are determined by the beam's physical state. A white beam produces a rich, fundamental tone. A red beam produces a warm, low-pitched harmonic. A green beam produces a clear mid-range tone. A blue beam produces a high, crystalline note. When beams overlap or combine, their tones blend harmonically. The result is that every puzzle, as you solve it, builds a unique chord progression that resolves when all targets are hit.


The generative system is built on a few simple rules. Pitch is determined by the beam's vertical position on the grid — beams near the top of the grid produce higher pitches, beams near the bottom produce lower ones. Volume is determined by beam intensity — a focused beam through a convex lens is louder and clearer; a split beam from a beam splitter is softer. Timbre is determined by the beam's color — red beams use a warm synth pad, green beams use a clean bell tone, blue beams use a glassy arpeggiator. When the player places a mirror and the beam bounces, there is a brief percussive "ping" at the reflection point. When a prism splits a beam, there is a crystalline shattering sound that blooms into the component tones. When the puzzle is solved, all tones converge into a single resonant chord that fades slowly — the musical equivalent of the visual bloom cascade.

Beam Tones

Each beam produces a sustained tone based on its color and grid position. White = rich fundamental. Red = warm low. Green = clean mid. Blue = crystalline high. Intensity controls volume.

Interaction Sounds

Mirror reflection = percussive ping. Prism split = crystalline shatter into component tones. Filter = soft absorption thud. Beam splitter = echo/delay effect. Target hit = resonant confirmation tone.

Ambient Bed

Beneath the generative tones, a very low, slowly evolving ambient drone provides harmonic context and fills silence during planning phases. Shifts key based on the puzzle's chapter.

Solve Chord

When all targets are hit, all active tones converge into a single sustained chord — different every time, always consonant, always satisfying. Fades over 3 seconds with the visual bloom.

⚠ Open Design Question

Should the generative audio system be harmonically constrained (ensuring all tones always form pleasant chords — pentatonic scale mapping, etc.) or fully free (any beam placement produces any pitch, potentially dissonant)? A constrained system guarantees a pleasant experience but sacrifices the sense that you're truly "composing." A free system is more authentic but could sound harsh. Proposed compromise: soft constraint — map grid positions to a pentatonic scale by default, but allow an "experimental mode" toggle that unlocks chromatic freedom.

06 — Enemies

Obstacles of Shadow

Enemies in Lumatical are not action threats — they are puzzle elements that occupy grid cells and must be dealt with as part of the solution. They do not move in real-time. They do not chase the player. They sit on the grid, blocking beam paths and sometimes emitting interfering light of their own, and the player must figure out how to neutralize them using the same tools they use to route beams to targets. Every enemy has a specific color weakness — hitting it with the correct color beam destroys it, clearing the cell and often revealing a new path or opening up routing options.

Shadow Block

The simplest enemy. Occupies a grid cell and absorbs any beam that hits it, regardless of color. To destroy it, you must hit it with any beam of sufficient intensity (requiring a lens focus or an unsplit beam). Shadow blocks teach the player that intensity matters, not just color routing. They appear as dark, slightly pulsing cubes on the grid — visually distinct from the neutral blockers that cannot be destroyed.

WEAKNESS: HIGH INTENSITY

Chromatic Shade

A colored enemy that blocks all beams except one specific color. A Red Shade absorbs green and blue beams but is destroyed by a red beam. These enemies force the player to think about color routing in a defensive context — you can't just get red to the red target, you also need to route red through (or around) the Red Shade that's blocking your path. Chromatic Shades appear as translucent, ghostly cells tinted their weak color, with a subtle glow indicating their vulnerability.

WEAKNESS: OPPOSITE COLOR BEAM

Null Emitter

The most complex enemy type. A Null Emitter sits on the grid and periodically emits a "null beam" — a dark, anti-light beam that cancels out any beam it intersects. The null beam follows a fixed, predictable path (shown as a faint dark line on the grid) and pulses on a visible cycle, so the player can see when it's active and when it's dormant. To destroy a Null Emitter, you must hit it with a beam while its null emission is dormant. These introduce a timing/rhythm element to otherwise static puzzles, but since the cycle is always visible and predictable, it remains a logic puzzle rather than a reflex challenge.

WEAKNESS: BEAM DURING DORMANT CYCLE

⚠ Open Design Question

How early should enemies be introduced? Three options:

  • Chapter II — After the player is comfortable with mirrors and prisms. Enemies appear as a new "obstacle type" alongside normal blockers.
  • Chapter III — After filters and lenses are introduced, so the player already has the tools to deal with color-specific enemies.
  • Optional encounters — Some puzzles within chapters have enemies, others don't. This prevents the game from feeling like it shifts genres midway.

Leaning toward Chapter III with optional encounters in earlier chapters as "bonus puzzles."

07 — Progression

Chapter Structure

Lumatical uses a linear chapter-based progression. Each chapter introduces one or two new mechanics and then explores them deeply through a series of escalating puzzles. The chapters are not gated by story (there is no story) but by mastery — completing enough puzzles in a chapter unlocks the next. Within a chapter, the player has some freedom: puzzles are presented in a linear sequence, but you can skip ahead by 1–2 puzzles if you're stuck, and return to skipped puzzles later. Completing all puzzles in a chapter unlocks a "Mastery Puzzle" — a single, fiendishly difficult puzzle that combines every mechanic from that chapter.

  1. Reflection Foundation

    12–16 puzzles. Introduces the grid, light sources, targets, and mirrors. The player learns how to route a single beam to a single target using reflections. Puzzles start with one mirror and one target, building up to multi-mirror routing through obstacle fields. By the end, the player is comfortable with beam direction and the concept of tool scarcity.

  2. Dispersion New: Prism

    14–18 puzzles. Introduces the prism and the concept of color. The first few puzzles are simple splits — white beam, prism, three targets. Then complications: what if the targets aren't aligned with the prism's natural divergence? What if you need to split the beam and then re-route two of the three colors? This chapter solidifies color as a core puzzle dimension.

  3. Refraction New: Lens + Filter Enemies

    16–20 puzzles. Introduces the lens (beam bending and focusing) and the color filter (color removal). This is also where enemies first appear — Shadow Blocks and Chromatic Shades that require specific beam colors or intensities to destroy. The puzzle complexity jumps significantly here, as the player now has six tools and two enemy types to reason about simultaneously.

  4. Duplication New: Beam Splitter

    16–20 puzzles. Introduces the beam splitter and the intensity mechanic. Splitting a beam halves its intensity — chain too many splits and the beams become too weak to activate targets. This forces the player to think about intensity as a resource, not just color and direction. Null Emitters appear here, adding the timing/dormancy mechanic.

  5. Convergence Advanced

    14–18 puzzles. No new tools. Instead, this chapter is about combining everything the player has learned in ways that require multi-step reasoning. Puzzles might require splitting a beam, filtering one component, re-routing another through a lens, bouncing both off mirrors, and converging them on a single target. These are the puzzles that make you stare at the grid for ten minutes before placing your first tool.

  6. Luminescence Final

    10–14 puzzles + Final Challenge. The hardest chapter. Large grids, minimal tools, multiple enemy types, and solutions that require non-obvious interactions (e.g., using a mirror to double-reflect a beam back through a prism you already used). The Final Challenge is a single, massive puzzle that uses every tool and enemy type in the game. Solving it unlocks a unique visual effect and the title "Luminary."

Skipping & Accessibility

Players can skip up to 2 puzzles per chapter without penalty. Skipped puzzles remain marked as incomplete and can be returned to at any time. This prevents hard walls while still encouraging players to engage with every puzzle. The Mastery Puzzle at the end of each chapter cannot be skipped.

Hint System

After 3 minutes of inactivity on a puzzle, a subtle "hint spark" appears — a faint glow on one of the grid cells suggesting a tool placement. The player can toggle hints on/off. Hints never reveal the full solution; they only point toward the next logical step. This keeps the puzzle satisfying while preventing frustration walls.

08 — Level Editor

Build Your Light

The level editor is not a post-launch afterthought — it is a v1 core feature and serves double duty as the team's internal puzzle creation tool. Every handcrafted puzzle in the game was built using the same editor the player has access to. This dogfooding approach ensures the editor is actually good: if it's frustrating for us to use, it's frustrating for players. The editor is accessible from the main menu and uses the same isometric grid view as the game, making the transition seamless.


The editor supports the full puzzle creation workflow: place a light source, place targets (specifying required color and intensity), place blockers and enemies, set the available tool budget (how many mirrors, prisms, lenses, etc. the solver gets), and then test the puzzle by playing it. The editor includes a solvability validator that runs the light simulation and confirms whether the puzzle is actually solvable with the given tool budget. It doesn't tell you how to solve it — just that a solution exists. This is critical for preventing the creation of impossible puzzles.

Sharing

Puzzles are saved as compact JSON files and can be shared via a short alphanumeric code (similar to SMM course IDs). Players can enter a code to download and play any shared puzzle. Steam Workshop integration for PC. On mobile, share via clipboard or QR code.

Programmatic Generation API

Alongside the GUI editor, Lumatical exposes a puzzle definition format (JSON schema) that can be generated programmatically. This enables two key workflows: (1) developers can batch-generate puzzle drafts using scripts and then curate/refine them in the GUI editor, and (2) the roguelike mode's procedural generator outputs puzzles in this format, meaning the same validation and play pipeline works for both handcrafted and generated content.

Validator

The solvability validator runs a beam simulation using the defined tool budget. It does NOT solve the puzzle (no AI solver) — it simply confirms that at least one valid beam path exists from source to all targets using the available tools and grid layout. If no path exists, it highlights the unreachable targets. If multiple paths exist, it does not rank them. This keeps the editor simple while preventing impossible puzzles.

⚠ Open Design Question

Should the editor support puzzle sequences (creating a chapter-like progression of connected puzzles) or only standalone single puzzles? Sequences would allow community creators to build full campaigns, but add significant UI complexity. Proposed: v1 ships with single-puzzle editing only, with sequence support added in a post-launch update based on community demand.

09 — Roguelike Mode

Infinite Light

The roguelike mode is Lumatical's answer to the question every puzzle game faces: what happens when the player finishes all the handcrafted puzzles? Rather than leaving the game as a finite experience, the roguelike mode provides procedurally generated puzzles that are different every time, giving the game effectively infinite replay value. This is not a random level generator slapped on at the end — it is a carefully designed system that produces puzzles which feel handcrafted by working backwards from a solution.


The core generation algorithm is solution-first: rather than placing random elements and hoping a puzzle is solvable, the generator starts by placing tools on the grid, running the light simulation to produce the resulting beam paths, and then placing targets at the beam endpoints. The obstacles and enemies are added afterward to create the challenge of routing the beams to those targets using the same (or fewer) tools. This approach guarantees solvability by construction — every generated puzzle has at least one valid solution because the puzzle was built from that solution.

Roguelike Structure

The roguelike mode is structured as a descent through floors. Each floor is a single puzzle. Completing a floor moves you to the next, which is slightly harder (larger grid, more targets, more enemies, tighter tool budget). Every 5 floors is a "boss floor" — a larger, more complex puzzle with multiple sources and targets. The run ends when the player fails to solve a puzzle (they can skip one puzzle per 10 floors, mirroring the main game's skip system). The player's score is the number of floors cleared, with bonuses for solving puzzles with fewer tools than the budget allowed (efficiency scoring).


Between floors, the player may be offered a modifier — a rule change that persists for the rest of the run. Examples: "All beams move 15% faster" (if we add a time element), "Mirrors can be rotated to any angle" (removes the 45-degree snap), "One extra tool per puzzle" (generosity), "No beam preview" (hardcore mode). Modifiers add variety and strategic depth to longer runs, making each run feel distinct even though the puzzles are randomly generated.


The difficulty curve is parameterized. The generator has knobs for grid size, number of sources, number of targets, number of required tool types, enemy density, and tool budget tightness. These parameters increase gradually across floors and spike on boss floors. The generation parameters are also influenced by the player's performance — if the player is solving quickly, the difficulty ramps faster; if they're struggling, it softens. This adaptive difficulty ensures the mode stays engaging without becoming either trivially easy or impossibly hard.

Solution-First Generation

By generating puzzles from solutions, we guarantee solvability without needing an AI solver. The generator places tools, simulates beams, and derives targets from the result. This is mathematically sound and computationally cheap — a single puzzle generates in under 50ms.

Difficulty Scaling

Parameters like grid size, target count, enemy density, and tool budget are tuned per floor. A difficulty curve function interpolates between "tutorial-easy" (floor 1) and "chapter VI hard" (floor 50+), with boss spikes every 5 floors.

Run Modifiers

Between-floor choices that add persistent rule changes. Some make the game easier (extra tools), some harder (no preview), some weirder (mirrors reflect at random angles). Adds roguelike variety and strategic decision-making to puzzle runs.

Leaderboards

Floor count + efficiency score. Daily seeded run (same puzzles for everyone) for competitive play. Weekly challenge with special modifiers. The seeded daily run gives the mode a communal, event-like quality that brings players back.

✓ Feasibility Assessment — Roguelike Random Puzzles

The solution-first generation approach makes this highly feasible. The key insight is that we never need to solve a random puzzle — we construct a puzzle from a solution. This sidesteps the hardest problem in procedural puzzle generation (proving solvability) and turns it into a straightforward simulation task. The main risk is puzzle quality: generated puzzles may feel same-y or lack the elegant "aha moment" of handcrafted ones. Mitigation: the generator uses template archetypes (narrow corridor, open field, enemy gauntlet, multi-source) to ensure structural variety, and the obstacle placement algorithm uses heuristics to create interesting routing challenges rather than trivial ones. We should prototype this early to validate the approach.

⚠ Open Design Question

Should the roguelike mode have a visual progression (the backgrounds and grid aesthetics change as you descend) or stay visually consistent throughout? Visual progression would give a sense of journey and make each floor feel distinct, but it increases the art/asset burden. Since we're using programmatic assets, the cost is primarily in shader work and parameter tuning — likely manageable. Leaning toward yes, visual progression — the grid shifts color palettes every 10 floors (cyan → magenta → amber → emerald), with the background becoming progressively more abstract and intense.

10 — Technology

Under the Hood

Lumatical is built in Godot Engine (v4.x), chosen for its excellent cross-platform support, lightweight footprint, and strong 2D/2.5D rendering capabilities. The game's visual effects (bloom, chromatic aberration, screen-space reflections) are implemented as Godot shader passes, keeping the rendering pipeline fully GPU-accelerated. The physics simulation for beam routing is custom-built — it does not use Godot's built-in physics engine, which is designed for rigid body dynamics. Instead, we implement a dedicated ray-based light simulation that traces beams through the grid, applying reflection, refraction, and splitting rules at each tool intersection.

Platform Considerations

PC (Steam) — Primary

Full shader effects (Vulkan/Forward+). Mouse-driven drag-and-drop. Steam Workshop for puzzle sharing. Steam achievements per chapter. Keyboard shortcuts for power users (rotate tool with R, undo with Ctrl+Z). Target: 60fps at 4K with full effects stack.

Mobile (iOS/Android) — Secondary

Touch-optimized drag-and-drop with haptic feedback on tool placement and puzzle solve. Compatibility rendering mode (reduced SSR, simpler bloom). Shorter puzzle sessions — the roguelike mode is the natural mobile fit. Adaptive layout for phones and tablets. No microtransactions — premium buy-once model.

Beam Simulation Architecture

The beam simulation is the technical heart of Lumatical. It runs as a deterministic, frame-independent step simulation: given a puzzle configuration (source positions and angles, tool positions and rotations, obstacle layout), the simulator traces each beam step-by-step through the grid, applying the appropriate transformation at each tool intersection. The simulation produces a list of beam segments (start point, end point, color, intensity, width) that the renderer draws as glowing neon lines with bloom effects.


The simulation runs at two resolutions: preview mode (fast, approximate — used during drag-and-drop tool placement to show a real-time beam preview) and full mode (precise, used when the player commits to a configuration and "fires" the beam). Preview mode caps at 2 reflections/splits to maintain 60fps during interaction. Full mode has no cap and traces beams to completion (absorption by blocker, exit from grid, or reaching a target). Both modes are implemented in C++ as a Godot module for maximum performance.


The simulation is also used by the level editor's validator and by the roguelike puzzle generator. This single simulation codebase serves three distinct features, ensuring consistency — if a puzzle works in the editor, it works in the game, and if a puzzle is generated as solvable, it is guaranteed to be solvable at runtime. This is a significant architectural advantage of keeping the simulation as a standalone, well-tested module rather than scattering beam logic throughout the codebase.

11 — Roadmap

Path to Launch

The development roadmap is organized into four phases, each producing a testable milestone. The total estimated timeline is 14–18 months for a small team (2–3 people), with Phase I focused on proving the core mechanics and Phase IV dedicated entirely to polish and content volume.

Phase I — Foundation

Months 1–4

Build the beam simulation engine (C++ Godot module). Implement the isometric grid renderer with basic beam visuals (no bloom yet). Create the drag-and-drop tool placement system. Implement mirrors, prisms, and color splitting. Build 20 prototype puzzles covering Chapters I and II. Validate the core loop: is it fun to route light? Can we make puzzles that are satisfying to solve? Milestone: internal playtest of the first 20 puzzles with raw visuals.

Phase II — Depth

Months 4–8

Implement remaining tools (lens, filter, beam splitter). Add enemies (Shadow Block, Chromatic Shade, Null Emitter). Build the full shader effects stack (bloom, chromatic aberration, SSR, particle drift). Implement the generative audio system. Build 40–50 puzzles covering Chapters III and IV. Create the level editor (GUI + validation). Milestone: alpha build with 60+ puzzles, full visuals, editor, and audio. External playtest begins.

Phase III — Infinity

Months 8–12

Implement the roguelike procedural generation system. Build the solution-first generator, difficulty scaler, run modifier system, and leaderboards. Complete Chapters V and VI with 30–40 advanced puzzles and the Final Challenge. Implement sharing (Steam Workshop, share codes). Mobile touch optimization and adaptive layout. Milestone: beta build with all content, roguelike mode, and cross-platform support.

Phase IV — Polish

Months 12–18

Content volume — fill out remaining puzzles to hit the 80–120 target. Iterate on puzzle quality based on beta feedback. Accessibility pass (colorblind modes, scalable UI, input remapping). Performance optimization, especially mobile shader fallbacks. Localization. Steam page, trailers, marketing. QA and certification for console submission (if pursuing post-launch). Milestone: release candidate. Ship on Steam. Launch mobile ports within 3 months of PC release.

Team Size

Minimum viable: 2 people (1 programmer + 1 designer/puzzle creator). Ideal: 3 people (add a dedicated audio/visual effects person). The programmatic asset approach keeps the art burden low — no need for a traditional 2D artist.

Early Priorities

Phase I is the make-or-break phase. If routing light with mirrors and prisms isn't satisfying by month 3, we need to reassess the core mechanic before investing in content volume. The prototype puzzles should prove the concept before we commit to the full puzzle count.

Playtest Cadence

Internal playtest at end of Phase I. External closed playtest (20–30 players) starting Phase II. Open beta in Phase III. Every playtest round includes both puzzle difficulty feedback and aesthetic/vibe feedback — the feel is as important as the function.
